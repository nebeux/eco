Learn the language
Key investing terms, explained
E
Environmental
The E in ESG measures how a company interacts with the natural world — carbon emissions, energy use, water consumption, waste, and how it's preparing for climate change. A high environmental score means the company is actively reducing its footprint.
A company that runs its data centers on 100% renewable energy scores higher on E than one powered by coal.
1 / 8
S
Social
The S covers how a company treats people — employees, suppliers, customers, and communities. Fair wages, workplace safety, diversity, data privacy, community investment. Companies with strong social scores tend to have lower turnover and fewer scandals.
A retailer that pays living wages and offers healthcare to part-time workers scores better on S than one that doesn't.
2 / 8
G
Governance
Governance looks at how a company is run at the top — board composition, executive pay, shareholder rights, transparency, anti-corruption. Good governance means the company is accountable and well-managed, which usually means fewer nasty surprises for investors.
A company where executive pay is tied to long-term performance, not just short-term stock price, scores well on G.
3 / 8
ESG
ESG Score
ESG stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance. Together these three dimensions give a picture of how responsible and sustainable a company is beyond its financial performance. Investors use ESG scores to find companies less likely to blow up from scandals, regulation, or climate risk.
A company can be highly profitable but have a low ESG score if it pollutes heavily, treats workers poorly, or has a corrupt board.
4 / 8
CO₂
Carbon Score
A carbon score measures how much a company contributes to greenhouse gas emissions — covering its own operations, the energy it buys, and its full supply chain. In Eco, a higher carbon score is better. It means lower impact on the climate.
A clean energy company that makes solar panels might score 90/100. An oil refinery might score 15/100.
5 / 8
P/E
Price-to-Earnings Ratio
The P/E ratio compares a stock's price to how much profit the company makes per share. A high P/E usually means investors expect strong future growth. A low P/E can mean the stock is cheap, or that growth prospects are weak. There's no universally good number — it depends on the industry.
If a stock costs $100 and earns $5 per share, the P/E is 20. Tech companies often sit above 30. Utilities often sit around 15.
6 / 8
β
Beta
Beta measures how volatile a stock is relative to the overall market. A beta of 1 means it moves in line with the market. Above 1 means it swings more — higher potential returns, higher risk. Below 1 means it's more stable but usually has lower upside.
A beta of 1.5 means if the market drops 10%, this stock tends to drop 15%. If the market rises 10%, it might rise 15%.
7 / 8
DIV
Dividend
A dividend is a regular cash payment a company makes to shareholders from its profits. Not all companies pay them — fast-growing ones usually reinvest instead. Dividends signal financial maturity. The dividend yield tells you how much you earn annually as a percentage of the stock price.
If a stock costs $100 and pays $3 per year in dividends, the yield is 3%. That's income on top of any price gains.

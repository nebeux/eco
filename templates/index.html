Learn the language
Key investing terms, explained
E
Environmental
The E in ESG measures how a company interacts with the natural world. This includes its carbon emissions, energy use, water consumption, waste management, and how it's preparing for climate change. A high environmental score means the company is actively reducing its footprint.
💡 Example: A company that runs its data centres on 100% renewable energy scores higher on E than one powered by coal.
1 / 8
S
Social
The S covers how a company treats people — its employees, suppliers, customers, and the communities it operates in. This includes fair wages, workplace safety, diversity and inclusion, data privacy, and community investment. Companies with strong social scores tend to have lower turnover and fewer scandals.
💡 Example: A retailer that pays living wages and offers healthcare to part-time workers scores better on S than one that doesn't.
2 / 8
G
Governance
Governance looks at how a company is run at the top. It covers board composition, executive pay, shareholder rights, transparency, and anti-corruption policies. Good governance means the company is accountable and well-managed — which tends to mean fewer nasty surprises for investors.
💡 Example: A company where the CEO doesn't also serve as board chair, and where executive pay is tied to long-term performance, scores well on G.
3 / 8
ESG
ESG Score
ESG stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance. Together these three dimensions give a picture of how responsible and sustainable a company is beyond just its financial performance. Investors use ESG scores to find companies that are less likely to blow up from scandals, regulation, or climate risk — and more likely to be around in 20 years.
💡 Example: A company can be highly profitable but have a low ESG score if it pollutes heavily, treats workers poorly, or has a corrupt board.
4 / 8
CO₂
Carbon Score
A carbon score measures how much a company contributes to greenhouse gas emissions. It covers Scope 1 (direct emissions from their own operations), Scope 2 (emissions from the energy they buy), and Scope 3 (the full supply chain). In Eco, a higher carbon score is better — it means lower impact on the climate.
💡 Example: A clean energy company that produces solar panels might have a 90/100 carbon score. An oil refinery might score 15/100.
5 / 8
P/E
Price-to-Earnings Ratio
The P/E ratio compares a company's stock price to how much profit it makes per share. A high P/E means investors are paying a lot for each dollar of earnings — usually because they expect strong future growth. A low P/E can mean the stock is cheap, or that the company's growth prospects are weak. There's no universally "good" P/E — it depends on the industry.
💡 Example: If a stock costs $100 and earns $5 per share, the P/E is 20. Tech companies often have P/Es above 30; utilities might sit around 15.
6 / 8
β
Beta
Beta measures how volatile a stock is relative to the overall market. A beta of 1 means it moves in line with the market. Above 1 means it swings more — higher potential returns but also higher risk. Below 1 means it's more stable, but usually with lower upside. Negative beta stocks (rare) tend to move opposite to the market.
💡 Example: A beta of 1.5 means if the market drops 10%, this stock tends to drop 15%. If the market rises 10%, it might rise 15%.
7 / 8
DIV
Dividend
A dividend is a regular cash payment a company makes to its shareholders from its profits. Not all companies pay dividends — fast-growing companies usually reinvest profits instead. Dividends are a sign of financial maturity and stability. The dividend yield tells you how much you earn annually as a percentage of the stock price.
💡 Example: If a stock costs $100 and pays $3 per year in dividends, the yield is 3%. That's income on top of any price appreciation.
